Project 1: Chess but we have PE

Assignment 1: Not-a-button

Ideation

For this assignment, I had an initial idea to use my tattoo as a button. My computer would recognize my tattoo with the web camera and would then open a webpage. After approximately 5 hours of trying to make a reliable system, I realized that it would be to hard for me to do, so I scrapped my idea.

My second idea I got was to create a chess game where you would select the square by moving your body along a ultrasonic distance sensor. Using two ultrasonic distance sensors would make two players collaborate with each other to choose a square and move the piece. The initial sketch can be seen on the right.


My initial sketch

Prototyping - Digital

To start off, I used digital tools to try the concept out. I did this for two reasons. First of, it is easier for me to create digital artifacts due to my disability. Second of all, I did not have access to an Arduino since I did not own any and every Arduino in MIDDLA was taken already.

I started using TinkerCAD, an online CAD service, since they have an Arduino simulator and a bunch of sensors and actuators that can be simulated. It also allowed Arduino code, which enabled me to produce working code that would be functional on an actual Arduino device. I created a simple circuit that connected two Ultrasonic Distance Sensors and wrote some code that would measure the distance in centimeters and map these to return a value between 1 and 8 (since there is 8 rows and 8 columns on a chess board). You can play with the circuit and the code here. (An account on TinkerCAD is needed)

In addition to the circuit, I wrote a really simple Processing script that simply drew a chess board and highlighted the selected square. Since I could not connect the Arduino simulator to Processing, I could not do anything more with it than just showing the concept.


The first version of the Processing sketch

Prototyping - Gone physical

While staying digital would be sufficient for the course, I felt like the interaction could not be experienced and therefore the hand in felt boring. Therefore I bought an Arduino to be able to implement it physically. I brought the Arduino to MIDDLA and Andreas helped me connect the circuit I made in TinkerCAD. However, there was only one Ultrasonic sensor available, so I had to work with that. I edited the code so I could send the data to Processing. You can see the interaction on the next slide.

I did some final polishing on the Processing sketch to make sure it looked nice. I also made it recieve data from the Arduino to be able to use that in my Processing sketch. Since I only had one sensor, I made that so you could move vertically and horisontally by implementing a simple timer that would change how the input would be used (vertically or horisontally). It was definately more janky than two sensors but it had to suffice due to the lack of sensors


The final version of the Processing sketch

Result and design critique

For the result, I am quite happy with the result. There is some issues with the sensor since it did not read always read the values accurately. There are mainly two potentional issues that could cause this. I did not do any data processing such as sampling, which could cause to inaccuracies. The sensor could also be broken, but the one I used was all new, so that should not be an issue unless it is a manufacturing issue. For a final version, this needs to be addressed.

The critique I got was good. The interaction felt interesting and self explainatory, which was nice to hear. I explained the original idea with two sensors, which they all understood. I got some critique about not using a proper ideation process, which would have helped me with my time. As mentioned, I tried to make an interaction with my tattoo, but when I realized that it was too inaccurate, I went back to square one and had no idea what to do. This chess idea was just an Heureika moment and I was just lucky that I got the idea. Otherwise, I'd probably be stuck and just trying to get something to hand in, which is a recipe for bad design. If I used proper ideation methods, I would have came up with several ideas that I could fall back to. I did not have this and was "just lucky" to get another doable idea in the time frame I had.

Project 2: Calming wristband using Deep Pressure Therapy

Assignment 2: On/around bodies & Assignment 3: Bridging theory and practice

The first parts (Assignment 2)

Ideation

For this project, me and my teammate met up and did some brainstorming. I did have an idea based on personal experience and Deep Pressure Therapy. The basic idea was a wristband that would hug the wearer to reduce stress. We took it a step further and set our target group to individuals with Autism, since they can be sensitive to impressions.

Our initial idea was to work with sound levels since people with Autism is known to be sensitive to loud sounds, but due to the lack of sensors, we switched to light instead. My teammate knew a person with Autism that was sensitive to high light levels, so we chose to focus on that.


The first initial sketch

Theory

The hug machine

Before discussing what Deep Pressure Therapy is, let us look where it all started.

Temple Grandin, born in 1947, is a scientist which was not diagnosed with autism until her 40s, and is one of the first people that documented how it was like living with autism (Barncroft, 2021). Her experiences led her to develop the Hug Machhine. It all started on her aunts' farm, where she noticed that cattles passing through the chutes was calmer when they had passed compared to before, and constructed a similar construction in hopes to calm down herself. The hug machine is a construction that squeezes the body evenly, providing pressure on the whole body. This is a tool used today to calm down people with autism.

Source: Bancroft. “The Calming Effect: Temple Grandin.” Bancroft, 24 June 2021, www.bancroft.org/resources/the-calming-effect-temple-grandin/.

What is Deep Pressure Therapy?

Deep Pressure Therapy is, simply put, the use of pressure in the form of stroking, hugging or any form of pressure on the skin (Karmara, 2021). The act of swaddling a baby to calm it down is a form of Deep Pressure Therapy. The most popular commercial product on the market utilizing this is weighted blankets.

On a more biological level, when deep pressure is applied to the skin, the sympathetic nervous system deactivates and the parasympathetic nervous system activates. The sympathetic nervous system is responsible for the "fight or flight" response and is activated during stress, fright and anxiety. The parasympathetic nervous system is the opposite, and triggers when you feel calmness. It releases Dopamine, which gives you the feeling of pleassure and satisfaction, and Serotonin, which contributes to a stable mood, happiness and even better sleep. Since deep pressure activates the release of these hormones, it is clear that the user will feel more calm.

Source: Karmara. “The Definitive Guide to Deep Touch Pressure Therapy.” Karmara, 11 Aug. 2021, karmara.co.uk/blogs/learn/deep-touch-pressure-therapy. Accessed 6 Mar. 2024.

Personal experience

As a person living with Cerebal Palsy since birth, I struggle daily with involuntary muscle movements and will probably do it all my life. While it is hard to explain how it feels to live with it, one can understand that it is energy consuming. I would like to categorize calmness into two categories, relative calmness and true calmness.

Relative calmness is a state where I am relatively calm and often look calm, but my muscles are still pretty stiff inside me, which one can feel if they would "investigate" my muscles. True calmness, on the other hand, is a state where even my muscles are calm and is "truly calm". This is a state that I strive for, but rarely is in.

Most of my calmness is in the relative calmness state, and to turn that to true calmness, tools such as weighted blankets is more often than not required to achieve that.

While this is not exactly related to the project, I think this gives more perspective to the design and highlights the true value proposition that the proposed design holds. I truly believe that there are room for research in this area, which I may or may not dive into in my master degree project.

Prototyping - The circuit

We started of by constructing our circuit that our wristband would use. The circuit consists of a light sensor and a 180 degree servo motor. The arduino was programmed so when the light sensor would record a value, the servo would turn. This servo would be responsible for the hug of the wristband. When we saw that the circuit worked as intended, we started to construct the wristband

Prototyping - The wristband

To remain simple and not commit too much to the design, we used a simple piece of cloth that we found in MIDDLA. We sew together the servo and the cloth to have them connected. The cloth became round like a wristband and could now be weared by the user.

The result of the prototype was a simple, somewhat weak but noticable wristband that had the intended effect. The only downside is that the servo motor needs to be held up in order to tighten the motor, otherwise the servo itself would spin around. All and all, a solid first prototype.

Design critique and reflection

The first design critique for this prototype went pretty well. We explained how Deep Pressure Therapy works to create more understanding of the purpose of the wristband. We then let our peers try it and even though the motor was pretty weak, they felt a noticeable tightning of the wristband when the light level threshold was reached.

The critique we got was that the design was interesting. One thing that contradicted our theory was that when the studio manager Dee came by and tried the wristband, she felt a bit stressed when the wristband tightened instead of calming down. It is worth noting that Deep Pressure Therapy is not foolproof and does not work for everyone. However, it is used to calm people with special needs down and does indeed work. By personal experience, we could maybe work with heat instead of hugs to provide another way of calming down the user. I know that heat is another way to calm myself down, but I do not know how it would work on our target group and needs to be explored further.

Within our critique group, we discussed that this is not limited to wristbands. This concept could be used in any piece of clothing to create "huggable clothing". One could go to the extreme and create a huggable suit that would hug the whole body. While we are not sure of how to build it, we could imagine it working simply by looking at our simple prototype. I think we really showed the concept really good and showed that our prototype can fill some space on the commercial market

Dee pointed out that this is something that could be taken further into a masters project and I think I will if I do not get another idea. I feel somewhat connected to the project since I could benefit from something like this and while there are commercial products already available, like weighted vests, they are quite bulky and does not really look good to wear. Further development in this project could lead to something that is both calming and nice-looking to wear. Current prodducts focus on the calming aspects, which in a certain degree is the most important thing, but the social aspects such as what it looks like is also important, and a poorly looking design used in public is very noticeable and not very discreete. I personally would not like to wear a bulky weighted vest when I am out and about, even if it might aid me, simply because of the looks of it.

The Arduino board is not really suitable for this project since it is quite big for wearables. Boards like Adafruit would suit better. But due to the lack of boards in MIDDLA, the Arduino had to suffice.

Further iteration (Assignment 3)

Disclaimer - Life happens

This sprint of work was affected by life. My teammate got ill and when we initially decided to sit down and work, I got a drop in slot at the optics when I was there to repair my glasses that I had to take. So it was difficult to find time to work, and it might be reflected in our final result. But as they say, life happens.

Ideation

We sat down and brainstormed how we should continue. We thought about skipping the Arduino and do a wristband that the user would be able to tighten it themselves when needed, but we did not know how to do that and that idea would be less interesting than a wristband that would hug the user in certain conditions, so we decided to stick to our current prototype. We both agreed that we needed to improve the wristband itself, as the cloth was very loose fitting and not super comfortable to wear, so all focus was made on the wristband. Of course, using a smaller circuit board and a stronger servo motor would improve our prototype, we did not have better alternatives available in MIDDLA and the shipping time for our desired components was too long to arrive in time. Therefore, we spent this week on the wristband and its material.

Prototyping - Improving the wristband

We bought a stretchy fabric that would sit more firmly on the wearers arm in the "rest" state, already providing a little bit of pressure to the user. This change in material created a better fit and allowed users of different arm sizes to still get a good and firm fit. It also provided a stronger hug when the wristband tightens. We thought about adding a small plastic shield inside thaé armband to strengthen the feeling of the hug, but we discarded that idea because it would be uncomfortable for the user compared to without it.


The improved wristband

The plastic shield we discarded

Design critique and reflection

Given that the design remained pretty similar to the last design crit, the critique we got was quite similar. Our peers liked how the wristband fitted and how the hugging felt. There was not any troubling about our design except for the construction, but that was due to the Arduino. For a commercial version, we really need to use a smaller boards intended for wearables. Being stuck to an Arduino board is not viable.

I think that due to the time clashes with my teammate, we might have achieved less than we could. This second design sprint was as mentioned spent on the wristband, which was quite simple. If we had more time, we could have worked on the circuit and try to improve the hugging mechanism. But as mentioned, we did not have access to stronger motors in MIDDLA, so we would have to order those ourselves.

To end it all, I am satisfied with what we achieved. Our prototype definately shows the concept and does allow for further iterations to a commercial product. I truly believe that this is something that people would benefit from.
